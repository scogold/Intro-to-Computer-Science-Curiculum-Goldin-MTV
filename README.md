# Intro-to-Computer-Science-Curiculum-Goldin-MTV
A high school Introduction to Computer Science curriculum designed for 9th and 10th grade students with no prior computer science experience. This curriculum is designed to expose students to computer science topics, build basic computer science skills, and prepare students to take AP Computer Science Principles in a following year. 

This course was developed by Scott Goldin at DSST Montview High School from 2024-2026

**Disclaimer this is a work in progress and will require modification to fit your classroom environment**

**The recommended way to use this curriculum is to follow along with the scope and sequence document for day-to-day lesson planning, and then use the materials in the folders that correspond with the daily lessons. The scope and seuqence is also attached on the readme for convenience.**

# Intro to Computer Science — Goldin

This Intro to Computer Science course is designed to be a one-semester course for 9th- and 10th-grade students. The goals of the course are as follows:

- Students will be introduced to computer science in breadth, learning a little bit about the many different fields of computer science.
- Students will gain a better understanding of how computers function, which will allow them to be better informed about how technology affects our world.
- Students will gain transferable skills to help them in a vast variety of career fields, not exclusively computer science.
- Students will gain a baseline understanding of computer science skills to help them as they advance to further courses in the field.
- Students will have fun and be exposed to positive experiences, leading them to consider pursuing STEM fields.

All materials not linked in the box, or otherwise specified, can be found in the folder for the corresponding unit.

- Students will understand that different number systems exist and follow similar patterns.
- Students will be able to convert between binary and decimal numbers.

| Day | Description | Materials |

**Goals:**
- Students will write programs using basic commands and define their own methods.
- Students will use control structures, such as loops and conditionals, to solve problems.
- Students will break larger problems into smaller, manageable steps to guide Karel through challenges.

For this unit, I use CodeHS resources for most of the daily lessons. In the attached folders are some on-paper Do Now worksheets to give throughout the unit, as you see fit. On-paper Do Nows are great for introducing the concepts before you need to teach the syntax.

## Unit 6 — AI

- Students will understand how AI developed over time, from early automation to modern large language models.
- Students will explain at a high level how neural networks learn from data.
- Students will train and evaluate a simple AI model of their own.

| Day | Description | Materials |

## Unit 7 — Game Development with Godot

**Goals:**
- Students will create 3D models and assets in Blender.
- Students will build a game in Godot using nodes, scenes, and scripts.
- Students will collaborate to plan, divide up, and produce a final game project.

| Day | Description | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| Day 1 | Show students how to download Blender (the Windows Store works best), then have them follow along with a YouTube tutorial. Pause the video intermittently to let students catch up, or use a class set of headphones so students can follow along independently. | Suggested Tutorial:<br>https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Ci3Has4L5W4 |
| Day 2 | Have students follow along with a second Blender tutorial; this will help them gain experience using the basic tools. | Suggested Tutorial:<br>https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=nESaz92SJ0w |
| Days 3–5 | Give students a mini project to start building Blender assets for their games. Allow them to form groups of 2 or 3 to divide up the work of creating the games. | |
| Day 6 | Show students how to download Godot and give a brief overview of how the program is used. Have a discussion about what they should create in Godot vs. Blender.<br><br>Demo using Godot on the screen and have students follow along. Cover the following topics. I would highly recommend watching several Godot tutorials yourself before teaching this lesson.<br>1. Download Godot<br>2. Create a project<br>3. Explain nodes and scenes<br>4. Create a 3D root node<br>5. Navigating 3D space (zoom, orbit, pan, move)<br>6. File system<br>7. Play scene<br>8. Add a camera<br>9. Create an object (CSG Box 3D)<br>10. Create characters | |
| Day 7 | Continue to demo the Godot features, including:<br>1. Importing Blender files into Godot<br>2. Sharing files between computers with Google Drive<br>3. Using default scripts<br>4. Basic modifications to the default script<br>5. Changing the controls<br>6. Attaching the camera to your characters<br>7. StaticBody3D for still objects | |
| Day 8 – End of Semester | Allow students to work independently on their final projects each day. Assign a participation grade each day to keep students on track. Encourage student collaboration and division of labor. | Intro to Computer Science Final Project Rubric |
| Finals | Have students play each other's games. Go around and grade each game. After students have tried all of the other games, let them vote on awards for each other, giving the winners extra credit. | |
